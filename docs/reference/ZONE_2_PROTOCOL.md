# Zone 2 Training Protocol

**Source:** [Evoke Endurance — Zone 2: A Comprehensive Look](https://evokeendurance.com/resources/zone-2-a-comprehensive-look/)  
**Author:** Scott Johnston  
**Coaching context:** [[zone2_training]]

---

## What Is Zone 2

Zone 2 is continuous aerobic exercise producing blood lactate at or below **2 mMol/L**. It provides the biggest stimulus for improving aerobic capacity and forms the base on which all higher-intensity work rests.

The original Polar 4-zone system anchored zones to lactate concentrations:

| Zone | Lactate |
|------|---------|
| 1 | ~1 mMol/L |
| 2 | ~2 mMol/L (Zone 2 ceiling = AeT) |
| 3 | ~3 mMol/L |
| 4 | ≥4 mMol/L |

---

## Intensity — How to Find Zone 2

**Not recommended:** % of max HR — Johnston says it has "a very slim chance" of individual accuracy.

**Correct approach:** Anchor to two metabolic events:

1. **Aerobic Threshold (AeT)** — top of Zone 2
2. **Anaerobic/Lactate Threshold (AnT)** — top of Zone 3

**Testing:** Use the **HR Drift Test** (detailed in *Training for the Uphill Athlete*, Ch. 5). Self-administered and free.

**Field proxies (less precise but useful):**
- Upper limit of comfortable nose breathing
- Able to maintain a reasonable conversation
- Historical Norwegian method: athletes wore warm-ups and would not sweat

---

## Session Guidelines

- **Duration:** 30 minutes to several hours per session
- **Duration is the biggest multiplier** — longer, lower-intensity sessions drive adaptation more than shorter ones
- Stay at or below AeT; drifting above adds fatigue cost without proportional aerobic return

---

## Volume Benchmarks

| Athlete type | Zone 2 as % of total training |
|---|---|
| World-class marathoners | ~10–15% |
| Recreational athletes | Higher % acceptable (lower neuromuscular fatigue per session) |

> **Important caveat on the elite figure:** World-class runners' Zone 2 pace (~4:40/mile) is metabolically aerobic but imposes extreme neuromuscular stress. Their low Zone 2 % reflects fatigue management, not de-prioritization of the aerobic base. Do not misread it as permission to skip Zone 2.

---

## Physiological Adaptations

- Increased mitochondrial density
- Enhanced aerobic enzyme production
- Improved capillary density in slow-twitch muscle fibers
- **Lactate shuttle:** lactate produced in fast-twitch fibers is transported to and metabolized by slow-twitch fibers aerobically

---

## Common Mistakes

1. **Treating Zone 2 as trivially easy.** For fit athletes it can be genuinely challenging. The intensity is low relative to max, not relative to effort.
2. **Copying elite training programs** without an established aerobic base — leads to overtraining and injury.
3. **Excessive HIIT without base** (Aerobic Deficiency Syndrome) — underdeveloped aerobic metabolism requires prioritized Zone 2 work before HIIT is productive.
4. **Impatience.** Aerobic adaptations "take months and years of consistent Zone 2 training to maximize. You can't rush these adaptations."

---

## Role in Overall Training Structure

Zone 2 is the **base of the pyramid**. The bigger the base, the taller the overall endurance pyramid can be built.

- HIIT is a supplement to — not a replacement for — aerobic base training
- Without an adequate aerobic base, HIIT yields fewer reps, lower volume, slower recovery, and higher injury risk
- As fitness improves, add "very easy Zone 1 days" to manage accumulated fatigue; consider cross-training to maintain aerobic stimulus without additional neuromuscular load

---

## Key Principle

> "The biggest mistake is not doing enough Zone 2, and the second biggest is doing too much of it at too high an intensity."

Adaptations are slow and cumulative. Consistent, properly-dosed Zone 2 over months and years is the mechanism — there is no shortcut.
