Seiler's 80:20 Rule

[Stephen Seiler’s 80:20 rule (polarized training) is based on a surprisingly simple premise: 80% of your training should be strictly low intensity (below the first ventilatory threshold, or VT1), and 20% should be high intensity (above the second ventilatory threshold, or VT2).

The hardest part of implementing this model isn't the complex math; it is the discipline required to keep the easy days truly easy so that the hard days can be truly hard, avoiding the moderately hard "black hole" (Zone 3 in a 5-zone model) that causes fatigue without maximizing adaptation.

Here is how to implement the 80:20 rule across different timescales, and how it shifts through various training blocks.

### 1. Daily Implementation

On a daily basis, the 80:20 rule is generally applied by categorizing the _entire session_ rather than slicing a single run into 80% easy and 20% hard.

- **The "80" Days:** These are your pure aerobic, easy runs. Heart rate stays firmly in Zone 1 or 2. If you encounter a steep trail, you hike to keep your heart rate down. This strict adherence to low intensity is exactly what maximizes fat oxidation and builds the aerobic engine required for long endurance efforts.
    
- **The "20" Days:** These are your interval or hill repeat days. A "20" session will naturally contain easy miles (your warm-up, recovery jogs between intervals, and cool-down), but because the _intent_ and physiological stress of the workout are high, the whole session is counted toward your 20% allocation.
    

_Note on Data Tracking:_ Because those hard sessions contain warm-ups and cool-downs, if you are looking at raw "Time in Zone" data pulled from a Garmin API, a perfectly executed 80:20 _session_ ratio will often look closer to 90:10 in raw _Time in Zone_ data.

### 2. Weekly Implementation (The Microcycle)

The week is the most practical standard unit for balancing the 80:20 ratio.

- **Session Counting:** If you run 5 days a week, 4 days should be strictly easy, and 1 day can be a high-intensity interval session. If you run 6 days, you might do 5 easy and 1 hard, or 4 easy and 2 hard, depending on how you categorize a long run with some tempo miles baked in.
    
- **Master Athlete Consideration:** For an athlete in their late 60s, the recovery cost of high-intensity work is much higher than for a 30-year-old. Often, a single, highly focused "20" session per week (putting the weekly ratio closer to 85:15 or 90:10) provides the necessary neuromuscular and VO2 max stimulus without outstripping the body's ability to recover and repair connective tissue.
    

### 3. Monthly Implementation (The Mesocycle)

A standard monthly cycle usually consists of three weeks of building volume/load followed by one week of recovery.

- **During the Build Weeks:** You maintain the 80:20 session distribution while total volume increases.
    
- **During the Recovery Week:** Total volume drops significantly (often by 30-40%). However, intensity should not drop to zero. You maintain the _frequency_ of the 20% work to keep neuromuscular sharpness, but you reduce the _duration_ of those hard intervals.
    

### 4. Seasonally & By Training Block (The Macrocycle)

The 80:20 rule is a guiding principle, not a rigid mathematical law. It should absolutely fluctuate depending on your current training block.

- **Base Phase:** * **Ratio:** Shifts closer to **90:10** or even **95:5**.
    
    - **Focus:** Massive aerobic development, tissue resilience, and fat adaptation. The vast majority of the time is spent doing long, slow distance. The tiny fraction of high intensity is usually just short alactic strides (15-20 seconds) to maintain running economy and neuromuscular firing without generating lactic acid.
        
- **Build Phase:** * **Ratio:** Moves to the classic **80:20**.
    
    - **Focus:** This is where traditional VO2 max intervals (e.g., 4x4 minutes hard) or heavy threshold work is introduced to raise your physiological ceiling.
        
- **Peak / Specificity Phase:** * **Ratio:** Remains roughly **80:20**, but the _nature_ of the 20% changes drastically.
    
    - **Focus:** The high-intensity work becomes highly specific to the upcoming event. When preparing for races with aggressive vertical profiles like the Broken Arrow Skyrace or the Waldo 100K, those 20% sessions shift away from flat, fast intervals on a track to steep, sustained uphill threshold pushes or aggressive downhill neuromuscular training.
        
- **Taper Phase:**
    
    - **Ratio:** Volume plummets, but the 80:20 ratio of the remaining volume holds steady.
        
    - **Focus:** Shedding fatigue while keeping the engine primed.
        

### Key Takeaways for Trail

When running trails, pace is a deeply flawed metric for calculating your 80/20 split because of vertical gain, technical footing, and altitude. A 12:00/mile pace might be a Zone 1 recovery jog on a flat fire road, but that exact same pace could push you deep into Zone 4 on a 15% grade. Strict use of a heart rate monitor and honest Rate of Perceived Exertion (RPE) are essential to ensure your easy trail miles aren't secretly logging as high-intensity stress.
