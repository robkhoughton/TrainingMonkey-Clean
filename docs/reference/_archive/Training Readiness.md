# Training Readiness

Resting Heart Rate (RHR) and Heart Rate Variability (HRV) are two of the most effective non-invasive metrics for peering into your autonomic nervous system (ANS). For endurance trail runners, they serve as a dashboard indicating how well the body is handling and adapting to both training load and life stress.

When tackling 40-mile weeks across steep, mountainous terrain, the physiological toll isn't just cardiovascular; it involves heavy muscular damage from eccentric downhill loading and significant central nervous system (CNS) fatigue. Here is how to use RHR and HRV to evaluate your daily and weekly training readiness.

### The Core Physiology: Sympathetic vs. Parasympathetic

Your ANS has two main branches:

- **Sympathetic:** The "fight or flight" system. It ramps up heart rate, prepares the body for stress (like a steep climb), and suppresses recovery functions.    
- **Parasympathetic:** The "rest and digest" system. It lowers heart rate, initiates recovery, and facilitates adaptation.    

**RHR** indicates the overall volume of work the heart is doing at rest.

**HRV** measures the variance in time (in milliseconds) between successive heartbeats. A "messy" or highly variable heartbeat means the parasympathetic system is active and rapidly adjusting to your body's micro-needs—a sign of excellent recovery.

### Establishing the Baseline

Daily absolute numbers are meaningless in isolation, especially because HRV naturally decreases as we age. As an endurance athlete navigating their late 60s, comparing your HRV to population averages is counterproductive. The only metric that matters is your personal **rolling baseline** (typically a 7-day average compared against a 30-day to 60-day average).

Measurements should be taken first thing in the morning under consistent conditions, or via a reliable wearable that tracks data during deep sleep.

### The Training Readiness Matrix

You can evaluate your daily readiness by looking at how your acute metrics (last 24 hours) compare to your normal baseline:

#### 1. Green Light: Prime Readiness

- **HRV:** Within or slightly above normal baseline.    
- **RHR:** Within or slightly below normal baseline.    
- **Interpretation:** Your parasympathetic system is dominant. You are absorbing your training well.
- **Action:** Proceed with high-intensity intervals, long mountainous efforts, or heavy trap bar deadlifts.    

#### 2. Yellow Light: Acute Fatigue (Sympathetic Dominance)

- **HRV:** Significantly below baseline.    
- **RHR:** Elevated above baseline.    
- **Interpretation:** Your fight-or-flight system is working overtime. This is a normal, expected response the day after a grueling trail session, a race, or poor sleep.    
- **Action:** Keep the volume light and the intensity purely aerobic (Zone 1 or 2). If you had a hard workout planned, push it back a day.    

#### 3. Yellow Light: Parasympathetic Hyperactivity (The "Deep Hole")

- **HRV:** Unusually high (spiking well above baseline).    
- **RHR:** Unusually low (dropping well below baseline).    
- **Interpretation:** This often tricks athletes into thinking they are incredibly fit. In reality, a massive spike in HRV coupled with suppressed RHR usually means the body is so deeply exhausted that the parasympathetic system has gone into overdrive to force recovery.    
- **Action:** Active recovery or complete rest. Do not undertake heavy loading.    

#### 4. Red Light: Systemic Overreaching

- **HRV:** Consistently suppressed (below baseline for multiple days).    
- **RHR:** Consistently elevated (above baseline for multiple days).    
- **Interpretation:** You are accumulating fatigue faster than you can clear it. If this trend lasts more than 3-4 days, you are entering the territory of non-functional overreaching or overtraining.    
- **Action:** Immediate rest or significant deload until the metrics normalize.    

### Contextual Factors for Trail Runners

Data must always be paired with subjective feeling. A wearable doesn't know the complete context of your environment.

- **Altitude:** If you are running at higher elevations than your body is acclimatized to, RHR will naturally rise and HRV will drop as the body works harder to oxygenate.    
- **Dehydration and Plasma Volume:** Heavy sweat loss without adequate replenishment (even if you are diligently calculating stoichiometric electrolyte mixes) reduces blood plasma volume, forcing the heart to beat faster (higher RHR, lower HRV) to maintain cardiac output.    
- **The "Age" Factor:** Older athletes often require a longer recovery tail after highly damaging sessions. While cardiovascular fitness might bounce back in 24 hours, the CNS and muscular systems might take 48–72 hours to reflect readiness in your HRV scores following long descents.    

Using RHR and HRV isn't about avoiding hard work; it's about validating when to apply the maximum stimulus and when to pull back, ensuring that every mile actually contributes to physiological adaptation rather than just accumulating fatigue.
