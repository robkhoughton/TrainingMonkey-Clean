# NEUROMUSCULAR TRAINING — HILL SPRINTS, STRIDES, AND RUNNING FORM

**Reference doc:** [[NEUROMUSCULAR_context]]

## Principle

Brief, maximal efforts with full recovery drive neural adaptation — recruitment of fast-twitch fibers, improved stride mechanics, better running economy — without accumulating metabolic fatigue. This is why neuromuscular work belongs in every training phase, including base.

## Hill Sprint Protocol

- **Effort:** Near-maximal — 95%+ of best effort
- **Duration:** 8–10 seconds per rep (stop before any speed loss)
- **Grade:** 20%+ — steep enough that form breakdown is obvious; stairs taken 2 at a time are acceptable
- **Recovery:** 2–3 minutes complete rest between reps (walking is fine; full recovery is the point)
- **Volume:** Start with 6 reps; continue until distance per rep drops noticeably from the first rep
- **Frequency:** 1× per week; can be appended to any easy aerobic session
- **Phase:** Year-round — safe in base, build, taper, and recovery reintroduction phases

Do not shorten recovery to add cardiovascular load. The metabolic cost of hill sprints is irrelevant — the neural recruitment pattern is the target.

## Strides

Strides are a lower-intensity neuromuscular complement to hill sprints. Operational details — count, duration, recovery, placement rules, phase-aware gating — are managed by the workout plan and injected per-session. When a session includes strides, follow the injected execution instructions.

Do not prescribe strides outside the workout plan's placement rules. In particular, never add strides the day after a long run or immediately after an interval session.

## Running Form — Overstriding

Foot strike should land directly under the body's center of mass. Reaching the foot forward before the body arrives (overstriding) creates a braking force on every stride, increases ground contact time, reduces efficiency, and raises injury risk.

Coaching cue when form degrades: "Shorten your stride and increase turnover — let your foot land under your hip, not in front of it."

Do not cue overstriding correction by prescribing pace or terrain. It is a mechanics correction only.
