# TRAIL RUNNING SPECIFICS

## Load Calculation
Trail load != road miles. Effective load = distance_miles + elevation_gain_feet / 750.
A 10-mile run with 2000ft gain = 12.7 load-miles. Use this when assessing ACWR and weekly targets.

## Eccentric Load and CNS Fatigue
- Downhill running causes 3–5× the eccentric muscle damage of flat running at equivalent pace.
- CNS fatigue from long descents outlasts cardiovascular recovery by 24–72h.
- DOMS from descents peaks at 24–48h; muscular readiness returns at 48–72h post effort.
- Do not schedule technical descents or intensity within 48h of a prior long descent effort.
- Two consecutive hard trail days (with vert) compounds CNS fatigue — avoid in weekly planning unless athlete is in peak fitness.

## Recovery Windows (Masters Athlete, 60s)
- After long mountainous effort (1500+ ft gain, 15+ miles): minimum 48h before next hard effort; 72h recommended.
- After race-level effort: 1 day easy per 10 miles raced is the floor; athletes in 60s often need 1.5× this.
- Cardiovascular recovery (HR, HRV) always precedes musculoskeletal and CNS recovery — do not use HRV alone to clear the athlete for intensity after a long descent day.

## Internal Load Is Always HR
- NEVER prescribe grade, terrain steepness, or elevation as a proxy for managing internal load.
- Internal load = HR only.
- To manage load on climbs: "if HR approaches VT1, shorten stride or power hike" — never "power hike climbs over X%".
- Terrain prescriptions are acceptable ONLY for injury management (e.g. avoid technical footing for ankle recovery).

## Uphill Hiking vs. Running

Above ~20% grade, running loses its flight phase and becomes biomechanically equivalent to hiking. Power hiking at that point is not a concession — it is the mechanically correct technique.

- Transition trigger: grade + accumulated fatigue, not grade alone. A 15% grade late in a long run may warrant hiking that would be runnable fresh.
- Prescribe hiking as a skill and technique, not as a fallback. Efficient uphill hiking is a trainable competitive advantage in trail/mountain running.

## Altitude Effects
- Unacclimatized: add +5–8 bpm to all HR zone ceilings; expect HRV suppression.
- Full cardiovascular acclimatization: ~1 week. Muscular efficiency: 2–3 weeks.
- Do not reduce volume at altitude — reduce intensity targets instead.
