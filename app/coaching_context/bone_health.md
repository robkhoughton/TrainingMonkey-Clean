# BONE HEALTH — HiRIT PROTOCOL FOR MASTERS RUNNERS

## Age-Tier Urgency

- **50–59**: Advisable. Sarcopenia and bone loss begin in this decade. Introduce HiRIT as bone insurance before the deficit compounds.
- **60–69**: Critical. Both processes are active and accelerating. HiRIT is a primary intervention, not optional supplementation.
- **70+**: Non-negotiable. Without high-magnitude loading, skeletal and muscular decline is the default trajectory. Every session counts.

Apply urgency language proportional to the athlete's age tier when making strength recommendations.

## The Endurance Runner's Paradox

High-volume trail running does NOT protect lumbar spine BMD. Ground reaction forces at easy-to-moderate trail pace rarely reach the osteogenic threshold (~4.2× body weight). The runner's skeleton adapts to linear, submaximal strain and stops responding. Masters trail runners commonly have LOW lumbar spine BMD despite high aerobic fitness.

Do not credit running volume as bone protection. It is not.

## Osteogenic Loading Requirements

Bone cells saturate after 36–50 loading cycles per session — additional reps provide zero further stimulus. What matters:
- **Magnitude**: ≥ 80% 1RM for compound lifts
- **Strain rate**: explosive concentric contraction; high rate amplifies the remodeling signal
- **Inter-rep rest**: 10-second pause between reps restores canalicular fluid flow, making each rep more effective than rapid sequences

## Minimum Effective Dose (MED) Protocol

- **Frequency**: 2× per week with 48–72h between sessions (bone resensitization window)
- **Structure**: 5 sets × 5 reps at 80–85% 1RM
- **Core lifts**:
  1. Deadlift (trap bar or barbell) — lumbar spine, posterior chain
  2. Back squat — femoral neck and hip (trap bar deadlift does not replicate femoral neck loading; back squat is required for hip BMD)
  3. Overhead press — upper thoracic spine, postural correction
  4. Drop landings — jump from ground, grasp bar, land firmly with slight knee bend; generates the high strain-rate impact stimulus
- **Session length**: 30–45 min

## Timing Rules

- Perform HiRIT before easy runs, not after — fresh bone is more responsive to mechanical loading
- No heavy strength within 72h of a target race
- No heavy strength within 48h of long descent efforts (compounded DOMS)

## Exercise Snack (Minimum Viable Dose)

When a full gym session is not possible: 10 maximum-effort vertical jumps, 3× per week. Evidence: +1–2% femoral neck BMD over 6 months. Prescribe as a gap-filler between HiRIT sessions, not a replacement.

## Pre-Race Taper — Bone Repair Extension

Extend pre-race taper to 3 weeks for athletes 55+. High-volume training weeks accumulate microdamage in bone matrix. Three weeks allows structural repair before race effort. This is a bone integrity requirement, not only a fatigue management measure.

## Pre-Exercise Calcium Buffer

For long efforts ≥ 90 min: recommend 1,000mg calcium (supplement or calcium-rich meal) 60 min before. Extended running drops serum ionized calcium → PTH spike → bone resorption. The pre-load buffer attenuates this response.

## Nutritional Targets

| Nutrient | Daily Target | Function |
|---|---|---|
| Calcium | 1,200 mg | Structural mineralization |
| Vitamin D | 800–1,000 IU | Calcium absorption |
| Vitamin K2 | 100–200 mcg | Activates osteocalcin to bind calcium to bone |
| Protein | 1.2–1.5 g/kg body weight | Muscle mass + collagen matrix |

Low energy availability (RED-S) suppresses estrogen, testosterone, and IGF-1 — all bone-building hormones. Athletes restricting calories for weight or performance goals carry compounded bone risk.

## Supervision Flag

At 80–85% 1RM, technical failure risk is significant and consequences are severe. When prescribing HiRIT, note that professional coaching or supervision is strongly advisable — most athletes self-select loads well below the osteogenic threshold without external accountability.
